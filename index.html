Marcel Marceau

"Music and silence... combine strongly because music is done with silence, and silence is full of music."

(1923-2007)

"Never get a mime talking, he won't stop."

  • Born Marcel Mangel, of Strasbourg, France, to a Jewish family
  • Fled to Limoges when France entered WWII
  • Father was seized by the Germans and died in Auschwitz
  • Adopted the last name "Marceau" during German occupation of France; after François Séverin Marceau-Desgraviers, a general of the French Revolution
  • Joined the French Resistance in Limoges, where he saved numerous children from the race laws and concentration camps. Making fake ration cards and smuggling children into Switzerland
  • With a command of the English, French, and German languages, he joined General George Patton's army as a liaison officer
  • Used mime for the first time to keep Jewish children quiet while he helped them escape to Switzerland
"Chaplin made me laugh and cry without saying a word. I had an instinct. I was touched by the soul of Chaplin — Mime is not an imitator but a creator."

  • Raised hearing the sound of wing beats from the pigeons his father raised on the roof. This sound enchanted him, so did silent films
  • "When I was five years old my mother took me to see Charlie Chaplin's moving pictures…. I sat entranced…. It was then that I decided to become a mime."
  • Imitated the famous comedian of the silent screen by borrowing his fathers pants and using ink to paint on a moustache
  • Began imitating birds, plants, trees, and eventually people
  • 1967 Met Chaplin for the first time between planes at the Airport. They expressed their mutual admiration.
"Do not the most moving moments of our lives find us all without words?"

  • Joined Jean-Louis Barrault's company cast in the role of Arlequin in the pantomime, Baptiste
  • Baptiste won him such acclaim that he was encouraged to present his first "mimodrama", Praxitele and the Golden Fish, at the Bernhardt Theatre the same year
  • Founded Compagnie de Mime Marcel Marceau
  • 1947 Created Bip the Clown, his most famous role
  • Silent mimed exercises: The Cage, Walking Against the Wind, The Mask Maker, and In The Park, became classic displays
  • 1949 - Received Deburau Prize, established as a memorial to the 19th century mime master Jean-Gaspard Deburau, for his second "mimodrama", Death before Dawn
"In silence and movement you can show the reflection of people."

  • Debuted in North America at the Stratford Festival of Canada
  • Known only by the intellectual minority until he toured the United States in 1955 and 1956
  • US tour ended with a record-breaking return to standing-room-only crowds in San Francisco, Chicago, Washington, D.C., Philadelphia, and Los Angeles
  • Appeared in motion pictures such as Professor Ping in Barbarella
  • 1976 - With purposeful irony, had the only audible speaking part in Mel Brooks' Silent Movie
"Life is a cycle, and mime is particularly suitable for showing fluidity, transformation, metamorphosis. Words can keep people apart; mime can be a bridge between them."

  • 1969 - Opened École Internationale de Mime in the Théàtre de la Musique in Paris
  • 1978 - Established École Internationale de Mimodrame de Paris
  • 1996 - Established the Marceau Foundation to promote mime in the United States
  • Marcel Marceau died at the racetrack in Cahors, France, on 22 September 2007 at the age of 84
